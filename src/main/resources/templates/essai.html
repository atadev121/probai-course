1 Introduction to probability

1.1 Real life example

Flow Ti is a first class company designing and producing pipes for many applications such as irrigation and waste management, Flow Ti is committed to deliver to its customers leak proof pipes with a probability of leaks at the field less than 5% with 99% certainty (as you may guess only God knows for sure if a pipe will or won't leak), for this it has required to its subsidiaries that all production lots of the pipes holding their trademark must undergo a hydraulic or leak test, by testing a sample from each lot and if some rejection threshold is reached (say 1 or 2 out of a sample of 10 pipes ) has leaked, 100% of the lot must be tested, for this, their quality manager has to set the sample size and the rejection threshold.

1.2 Population

Before going any further in this example, let's consider the concept of population, in the context of Flow Ti pipes a population of pipes is a set of pipes produced under same set of characteristics (production conditions, raw materials, work force, process configuration..), to be more precise let's say a population is much related to a production lot, we start a production run (or order) which may last for weeks, we suppose that production characteristics are more likely to drift away or simply change from the standard with time, so within a reduced laps of time those characteristics are more likely to stay the same, so a production lot may correspond to a quantity of 100 pipe or the production of one shift if we need more than one shift to produce 100 pipes. For the sake of simplicity let's suppose that we are producing pipes of small diameter, and that we need less than one shift to produce 100 pipes, so a population correspond to a production of 100 pipes. In the same production run we produce 100 pipes within some production conditions which corresponds to some quality level and we mean by quality level, leak rate say 8%, lets say this population 1(p1), after this 100 pipes, the guy in the winder machine did some adjustment and quality goes up, which corresponds to another population say population 2 (p2) with a rejection rate of less than 4%. Now suppose that we're producing non stop under conditions of population1, that is we are producing pipes with a rejection probability of say 8%, let's do an experiment, take 10 pipes every day and test them.

1.3 The coin toss trap

The pipe experiment described above is exactly the same as tossing a die ten times, and note how many heads and tails (for example 5 out of 10) and repeat the experiment many times , as you may guess the number of heads (good result, or good pipe, successes ) depends of the probability of having a head when you toss the coin (one time) but also on how many times we tossed the coin (for this experiment it's 10)

1.3.1 Tossing a biased coin n times

Let's do an experiment, toss a biased 📘 a biased coin is a coin with the probability of getting head and tail not the same, if we toss a fair coin, the probability of having a head is the same as the probability of having a tail which is 50% or 0.5 coin n times and note how many heads we get. 📘 getting a head is called a success in the probability terminology, because this what we're concerned about, this has nothing to do with whether or not it's a "good" thing, note that for Flow Tit it's a failure to have a pipe with leak
Suppose that the probability of having a head in one toss is p. 📘 getting a head is like finding a pipe with leak in the Flow Ti case, for the pipe problem, n=10 and p=8%, or 0.08

1.3.2 Distribution

Tossing the coin n times is called a trial, let's do many trials and note the number of heads in each trial, the result of each trial is of course random, it can vary from 0 to n, but it depends most certainly on n and p, for the sake of simplicity let's suppose that our coin is fair that is p=0.5, so having the majority of trials with no heads or no tails is not very probable, for example in 10 tosses we expect to have almost as many heads as tails, this guess is not valid for each trial, but for the whole set of trials, let's see how..

Analysis of the distribution:

Let's denote by X the random variable associated to the number of heads:
1/ The number of possible values that X can take is equal to n,   📘 for example for n=10, this number is 10, for n=100 this number is 100, because when you toss a coin 10 times, you can have no heads, one head, 2,3..or 10 heads, so the graphic above representing the histogram of number of heads in k trials contains theoretically n values,
2/ The values that the random variable X can take are concentrated around the expected value of X.   📘 this more clear for bigger values of the expected value of X (the expected value of X is not the value of X, for example if n=100 and p=0.3, X can be any number form 0 to 100, but the expected value of X as the term "Expected" may suggest is 100*0.3=30 ) for example for n=100 and p=0.5, the expected number of heads is 50 so values that are far from this value (0,1,2, 98,99,100), are almost certain not to have them as the outcome (because the expected value of X is 50), and the probability to have such values gets smaller as those values gets further from the expected value
